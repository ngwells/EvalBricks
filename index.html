The Problem

The Modern Youth Sports Tryout Is Operationally Broken

1. Impossible Multitasking

During a drill or scrimmage, one evaluator must simultaneously:

  • • Track each athlete’s jersey number
  • • Observe multiple players at once
  • • Record notes on paper
  • • Look down, look up, and not miss anything
  • • Remember context about positions, strengths, and weaknesses

This is cognitively unrealistic. Human attention is limited, and paper‑based workflows force evaluators to choose between watching the play or writing the note.

2. Fragmented, Unreliable Data

Old-fashioned pen-and-paper systems introduce structural failures:

  • • Notes are inconsistent across evaluators
  • • Handwriting varies, gets messy, or is incomplete
  • • Observations lack timestamps or drill context
  • • Data is scattered across multiple sheets
  • • No way to validate accuracy or bias

The result: tryout data becomes subjective, incomplete, and vulnerable to memory errors.

3. Error-Prone Tracking

Evaluators must constantly map: Player → Jersey number → Position → Drill → Performance notes.

One misread jersey number or rushed scribble can misassign an entire evaluation. Multiply that across 40–100 players and the error rate skyrockets.

4. Roster Creation is Guesswork

When it’s time to build a roster, evaluators must:

  • • Remember which notes belong to which player
  • • Infer which players fit positional needs
  • • Reconcile multiple sheets of paper
  • • Rely on memory (“Oh yeah, I remember that kid…”)

This is where the system completely collapses. Coaches end up selecting players based on impressions rather than evidence.

5. Matching Needs vs. Talent

A roster isn’t just “pick the best players.” It’s filling gaps, balancing strengths, building chemistry, and ensuring tactical coverage.

Paper notes do not support structured decision-making. They do not map traits to positional requirements, and they do not help evaluators compare players objectively.

6. Assumes Perfect Memory

The entire workflow implicitly relies on the evaluator “remembering everything.” That’s unrealistic and unfair. This is why teams often end up with:

  • • Misplaced players
  • • Overlooked talent
  • • Bias toward players who stood out visually
  • • Inconsistent evaluations across coaches

The system isn’t just outdated—it’s fundamentally incapable of producing reliable outcomes.
